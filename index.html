About Me

Hello, and thank you for stopping by! My name is Mariona Badenas-Agusti and I am a Ph.D. student in Planetary Sciences at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT). At MIT, I work with Prof. Sara Seager and Prof. Julien de Wit on exoplanet research.

A Little About My Work

Within planetary science, my primary interests are the discovery and characterization of multi-planet systems, eccentric and short-period eclipsing binaries, stellar activity and variability (with a focus on stellar pulsations of Delta Scuti and Gamma Doradus stars), and the study of exoplanet atmospheres with multi-wavelength transmission spectroscopy.

As an observational astronomer enthusiastic about efficient and powerful code, I greatly enjoy combining real telescope observations with my own programming tools in order to improve our understanding of distant, exotic worlds. I am a big advocate of open source science, open knowledge, and good data visualization practices.

Please visit the "Research" and "Publications" section of this website if you are interested in learning more about my work.

A Little About My Story

I began my Ph.D. in the Fall of 2019 as an MIT Presidential Fellow. Before embarking on this adventure, I pursued my undergraduate studies at Yale University, where I graduated with a B.Sc. in Astrophysics in 2016. For my undergraduate thesis at Yale, advised by Prof. Debra Fischer, I wrote a complete and automated analysis pipeline to characterize planetary systems from NASA's Kepler-2 Mission.

Upong graduation, I joined GTD Systems & Software Engineering as a R&D Space Engineer, where I worked until 2018. In this role, I contributed to the ALTAIR project for the European Commission, aimed at evaluating the feasibility of a reusable, green, lightweight rocket with the capacity to launch small satellites into Low Earth Orbit (400-1000km).

In parallel to my work in the space sector, I decided to delve deeper into astronomy with a M.Sc. in Astrophysics, High Energy Physics, and Cosmology from the Universitat Autònoma de Barcelona (UAB) and the Institute of Space Studies of Catalonia (IEEC). For my master’s thesis with Prof. Ignasi Ribas, I wrote a Bayesian routine to predict the orbital period of a transiting planet based on a single transit event, and extract the planetary and stellar properties of each individual transit.

Some Final Words

Throughout my career, I have had the chance to work and interact with brilliant individuals and renowned exoplanetary scientists. To them, I am incredibly grateful for their mentorship and support, as well as for instilling in me the importance of giving back to society.

One of the ways I try to contribute to my community is by sharing my passion for space through educational and outreach activities. I have given talks to a variety of audiences, both technical and non-technical, and collaborated on projects designed to illustrate the importance of science in society and our daily lives.

When not talking about exoplanets or looking up at the night sky, I can be found flying small planes, learning about astrophotography, and cooking.

I am a member of the Space Generation Advisory Council (SGAC) and Women in Aerospace-Europe (WiA).
